SgrB2 Large Scale: VLA Multi-configuration Mapping

Fanyi Meng | Fanyi's GitHub Pages

The giant molecular cloud complex SgrB2 is the most massive region with ongoing star formation in the Galaxy. It is located at a distance of 8.5 kpc from the Sun (Reid et al. 2014), and within 100 pc in projected distance to the Galactic Center (Molinari et al. 2011). Its high densities (>105 cm−3) and temperatures (∼50--70 K), together with its proximity to the Galactic center, make SgrB2 an interesting environment of extreme star formation, different from the typical star forming regions in the Galactic disk, but similar to the active galactic centers that dominate star formation throughout the Universe at high redshifts.

The whole SgrB2 complex comprises a total mass of ∼107 M⊙ (with ∼107 L⊙), distributed in a large envelope of ∼22 pc in radius (Lis & Goldsmith 1989). Despite the large mass reservoir, star formation seems to be mainly occurring in the two hot cores SgrB2-N and SgrB2-M. These two sites of active star formation are located at the center of the envelope, occupy an area of around 2 pc in radius, contain at least ∼50 high-mass stars with spectral types from O5 to B0, and constitute one of the best laboratories for the search of new chemical species in the Universe. The masses of SgrB2-N and M (5×104 M⊙ and 3×104 M⊙), however, correspond to only a small fraction (∼1%) of the total mass of the cloud. Furthermore, the current stellar mass is estimated to be 5×103 M⊙ (Belloche et al. 2013), while assuming a conservative star formation efficiency of 5%, one expects a factor 10--100 more stars to form. One wonders if all the new stars will form only in the central cores (resulting in one or two rich star clusters), or if the envelope will fragment and form stars spread over the whole region.

Understanding the structure of the SgrB2 molecular cloud complex is necessary to comprehend the most massive star forming region in our Galaxy, which at the same time provides an unique opportunity to study in detail the nearest counterpart of the extreme environments that dominate star formation in the Universe.
